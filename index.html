Notetime Note tool

Date: Fall 2017

As I was collecting articles on the history of the NYC subway fare, I found myself wanting a different way to save my notes. I was tired of reorganizing a Google Doc over and over. What I was looking for was:

  • tagging - the ability to view subsets of a notes set
  • dated - the ability to sort subsets of notes
  • portable - so many online note tools seem great but don't improve or fit your ideal data model; I didn't want to get locked in
  • publishable - the ability to quickly share a note I had just written

The result is a Node.js library which accepts a series of Markdown-based notes and outputs the grouped and sorted notes. The result is browseable on GitHub or through a docs integration (I used GitBooks).

notetime is on npm and GitHub. I am actively using it to compile my urban history notes.

Transit Network Centrality

Date: Fall 2016

After implementing basic graph algorithms on the New York City subway network, I began exploring centrality metrics on the New York City, Boston, Paris, and Washington, DC, subway networks. This lets us see what are the most important stations in the network and compare network structure across cities.

Check out my blog posts (first and second) on the centrality adventure and check out the results in the web app.

New York City Transit Frequency Visualization

Date: February 2016

I created a web tool to visualize New York City transit frequencies using Transitland and Mapbox. Schedule data from buses and subways (and even a few ferry routes!) is plotted for an example Friday and Saturday morning.
